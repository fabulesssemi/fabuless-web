TITLE: ASML Dilemma: High-NA EUV is Worse vs Low-NA EUV Multi-Patterning
SOURCE: SemiAnalysis - Dylan Patel
DATE: 2023-12-11
URL: https://www.semianalysis.com/p/asml-high-na-euv-dilemma

High-NA EUV single patterning costs significantly more than low-NA EUV double patterning for all upcoming process nodes including 1.4nm/14A. Furthermore, low-NA multi-patterning achieves finer feature pitch than high-NA single exposure. ASML's goal of 20 High-NA tools per year by 2028 is unrealistic.

High-NA Architecture Tradeoffs

To increase lens size (numerical aperture from 0.33 to 0.55 NA), ASML faced two choices: increase mask size (impractical — entire mask infrastructure uses 6" standard) or reduce exposure field size by half. They chose the smaller field. Result: die layouts must accommodate both half-field high-NA and full-field low-NA layers on the same wafer — complex mask planning, potential die size cost penalties.

Dose vs. Throughput — The Core Problem

Dose requirements increase exponentially as critical dimensions (CD) shrink. High-NA achieves smaller features by using a larger lens aperture, but still must print sub-3nm features — requiring extremely high dose that even 1kW source power cannot compensate.

Low-NA double patterning: prints features at 2x the final CD per exposure. Much lower dose required per pass. Despite requiring 2 scanner passes per layer, the lower dose enables each pass to run at full stage-speed-limited throughput (~200+ wafers per hour), not dose-limited (~80 wafers per hour for high-NA). Total cost: lower despite 2x passes.

Cost Model Results

Costs normalized to low-NA 3nm: Low-NA double patterning is cheaper at every node from 3nm through 1nm. High-NA becomes cost-competitive only at ~7A-10A nodes (2030+) when horizontal scaling gives way to 3D stacking — at which point CD shrink slows, dose requirements plateau, and high-NA throughput improves.

If source power cannot reach 1kW (due to optics degradation), lithography costs increase ~20% at future nodes vs. today — high-NA economics worsen further. Even with 1kW, high-NA is dose-limited at all nodes through the end of the decade.

ASML public materials shifted in 2021 from claiming "high-NA is lower cost" to claiming only "high-NA reduces process complexity." This is a tell — complexity reduction does not drive fab capex decisions; cost per wafer does.

Faster stages developed for high-NA will be backported to future low-NA tools — further improving low-NA economics.

Additional High-NA Challenges

Half-field stitching: chips must be designed around a half-reticle field for critical layers, creating mask layout complexity. Die size penalties possible for chips near the reticle limit.

Depth of focus: High-NA lenses have shallower depth of focus — tighter wafer flatness and topography control required.

Photoresist sensitivity: Higher dose still needed relative to low-NA for equivalent CD — resist chemistry at the edge of performance. Stochastic defects increase with smaller features and higher NA.

Samsung's Cautionary Tale

Samsung adopted EUV for DRAM at D1Z generation ahead of Micron and SK Hynix — suffered yield problems that reversed Samsung's DRAM density leadership. Micron, using DUV multi-patterning, now has the world's densest DRAM. The parallel for high-NA is direct: adopting it before economics justify it can destroy yield and cost position.

Investment Implication

ASML's high-NA ramp will be slower and less economically compelling than marketed. 20 tools per year by 2028 is extremely unlikely. Intel (Intel 14A) and TSMC will use high-NA for specific critical layers while continuing heavy low-NA multi-patterning — limiting incremental high-NA tool demand. Low-NA ArFi tools remain the workhorse of leading-edge fabs through the late 2020s. Directed self-assembly (DSA) at Intel 14A may provide a path to reduce dose requirements by 50%+ — most important enabler for making high-NA economically viable.

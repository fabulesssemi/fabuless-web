TITLE: GPU Cloud Economics Explained – The Hidden Truth
SOURCE: SemiAnalysis - Dylan Patel
DATE: 2023-12-04
URL: https://www.semianalysis.com/p/gpu-cloud-economics-explained

GPU clouds are significantly easier to operate than general-purpose clouds — no advanced database services, block storage, security guarantees for multi-tenancy, or complex virtualization. This low barrier to entry explains the explosion of 12+ new pureplay GPU clouds whose equity/debt proposals crossed SemiAnalysis desks in 2023.

CPU vs GPU TCO — Why GPU Clouds Work

CPU servers: Hosting costs (~$220/month) are roughly equal in magnitude to capital costs (~$301/month). Hyperscalers' lower PUE and datacenter efficiency advantages meaningfully impact CPU cloud economics.

GPU servers (H100 HGX): Hosting costs (~$1,871/month) are dwarfed by capital costs (~$7,025/month). Nvidia's massive margins mean GPU servers are 20x+ more expensive than CPU servers per unit. The capital cost dominates so completely that a "relatively poor datacenter operator" can still build a competitive GPU cloud.

PUE Impact: Hyperscale facilities approach PUE of 1.1. Colocation facilities average 1.4+. Newest GPU-purpose-built facilities target ~1.25. This difference is critical for CPU economics (hosting cost = large % of TCO) but nearly irrelevant for GPU economics (hosting cost = small % of TCO). A 15% hosting cost advantage for hyperscalers is worth ~$280/month on GPU vs $33/month on CPU.

H100 Cloud Pricing

All-in cost for a colocation GPU cloud provider with 13% cost of capital: ~$1.525/GPU/hour. Market pricing: $2-3/GPU/hour for H100. This implies ~30-100% gross margins for cloud providers at today's pricing. CoreWeave reportedly pitched customers on 8-year depreciation periods — "utter nonsense" per SemiAnalysis.

Key Cost Variables

Cost of capital (hurdle rate): Most important variable. 5% vs. 13% debt cost changes all-in GPU cost by ~15%.

Depreciation period: 3 years is standard. Extending to 4-5 years reduces monthly capital cost but increases residual value risk as Nvidia releases new generations annually.

Utilization rate: At 85% utilization, H100 costs ~$1.90/GPU/hour all-in. At 60%, cost rises to ~$2.70/GPU/hour. High utilization is critical — idle GPUs are pure cash burn.

Networking: InfiniBand switches for large-scale training clusters add $200-400/GPU/month. For inference-only deployments, networking costs are minimal.

Power density and cooling: GB200 NVL72 at 120kW/rack requires DLC (direct liquid cooling). Air-cooled alternatives (GB200A NVL36 at 40kW/rack) fit existing infrastructure at lower capex. DLC adds ~$50-100/GPU/month in infrastructure cost but enables higher rack density.

Hyperscaler Disadvantages

Hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP) are actually disadvantaged for GPU cloud economics because:
1. Their massive general cloud infrastructure runs at lower utilization than purpose-built GPU clouds
2. They pay full retail for Nvidia GPUs unlike preferred customers like CoreWeave who get allocation priority
3. GPU cloud software is simpler to operate, negating hyperscale software moats
4. GPU workloads need fewer value-add services (storage, databases, networking)

Third-party GPU clouds with cozy Nvidia relationships (CoreWeave, Lambda, Crusoe) receive better GPU allocations and can undercut hyperscale pricing.

Investment Implication

H100 cloud economics are attractive at today's pricing ($2/hour vs $1.52/hour all-in cost). The moat is not technology or infrastructure — it is GPU allocation relationships with Nvidia. Providers that Nvidia favors (by buying NVLink switches, optics, and committing to multi-year orders) get supply. Those that multi-source or build custom silicon face allocation penalties. The GPU cloud boom will end when Nvidia supply normalizes and GPU pricing drops — likely 2025-2026 as CoWoS capacity expands.
